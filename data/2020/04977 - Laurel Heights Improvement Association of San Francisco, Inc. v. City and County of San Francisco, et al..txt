4 Environmental Setting

5 10. The 10-acre project site at 3333 California Street is in the Laurel Heights

6 neighborhood. Presidio Heights lies to the north. Pacific Heights and the Western Addition lie
to the east and portions of the Laurel Heights area and the Anza Vista area of the inner
7 Richmond lie to the south. Built in 1957 on the site of the former Laurel Hill Cemetery, a
8 354,000 square-foot, a horizontal four-story International Style building and 13,000 square-foot

9 annex building on the site were designed by master architect Edward Bradford Page (1905-

10 1994) for the Fireman's Fund Insurance Company's new corporate headquarters. Landscaping

11 is an integral part of the original design by the highly-acclaimed master landscape

12 architectural firm of Eckbo, Royston & Williams.
11. The Fireman's Fund building dramatically transformed the former cemetery
13 site and stands as the most prominent postwar commercial development in the Laurel Heights
14 neighborhood. The building retains integrity in all seven categories of historicity - location,

15 setting, design, materials, workmanship, feeling, and association. It is eligible for the National

16 Register of Historic Places and is listed in the California Register of Historical Resources.

13 CEQA requires agencies to conduct a prescribed, public EIR process before

14 taking actions that may have a significant adverse environmental impact. The point of the

15 process is to inform the public and to provide necessary information to allow elected officials

16 and decisionmakers to comply with CEQA's substantive mandate: to adopt feasible project

17 mitigations and alternatives that can reduce a project's significant environmental impacts.
24. The city abused its discretion and failed to act in the manner required by law in

18 approving the 3333 California Street Mixed Use Project with significant environmental

19 impacts, inter alia and as documented and supplemented by the to-be-lodged record of
proceedings, including:
20

21 a. Certification of an inadequate EIR without a stable, accurate, finite project

22 description; with overly-narrow project objectives; with insufficient analysis of project

23 impacts, mitigations, and alternatives as a matter of law to adequately provide information to
the decisionmakers and the public (e.g., Sierra Club v. County of Fresno (2018); with inadequate

24 analysis of the revised project; with inadequate analysis of shadow impacts; failing to analyze

25 project inconsistencies with the city's general plan priorities, goals, policies, and elements; and
with inadequate analysis and mitigation for project impacts on biological resources,
26 Il construction noise, and the integrated historic architecture and landscape;

27 b. Failure to adequately respond to comments on the EIR;
